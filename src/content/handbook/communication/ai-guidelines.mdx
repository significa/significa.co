AI tools are part of how we work. That's not a policy position — it's just true. The question isn't whether to use them, but how to use them well.

This page sets out our thinking on that. It's not a rulebook. It's a shared framework for making good judgements.

## Our stance

We're pragmatic about AI. We don't fetishise it, and we don't avoid it. If a tool helps you do better work, think more clearly, or move faster without cutting corners, use it.

What we won't accept is AI as a substitute for thinking. Using a language model to generate a client proposal, a design rationale, or a piece of code you don't understand isn't a shortcut — it's a liability. You're putting your name on it. Make sure you can stand behind it.

Maximum freedom, maximum responsibility applies here too.

## What we encourage

**Using AI to accelerate, not replace**
AI is genuinely useful for drafting, summarising, researching, generating options, and getting unstuck. Use it to move faster through the parts of the work that don't require your best thinking — so you can spend more of yourself on the parts that do.

**Iterating with AI, not accepting the first output**
The first thing a model produces is rarely the right thing. Treat it like a first draft from a junior collaborator: useful raw material, not finished work. Push it, reshape it, make it yours.

**Using it to challenge your own thinking**
Some of the best uses of AI aren't about output at all — they're about process. Ask a model to steelman a position you disagree with, identify gaps in a plan, or critique something you've written. Use it as a thinking partner, not just a word generator.

**Experimenting openly**
If you've found a workflow, prompt, or tool that's genuinely changed how you work, share it. Drop it in Slack, bring it up in a team session. We all benefit from each other's experiments.

## What we don't accept

**Passing off AI output as your own craft**
There's a meaningful difference between using AI to assist your work and presenting AI output as your work. The former is a tool. The latter is misleading — to clients, to colleagues, and to yourself.

Client-facing writing, design decisions, code, and strategic thinking should reflect your judgement and your voice. AI can be in the process. It shouldn't be the process.

**Using AI on confidential client information**
Do not paste client data, project details, proprietary briefs, or anything shared under NDA into public AI tools. This includes ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and any other tool that isn't explicitly approved for confidential use.

If you're unsure whether a tool is safe for a given piece of information, assume it isn't, and check with your Team Lead before proceeding.

**Generating work you can't explain or defend**
If you used AI to produce something — code, copy, a recommendation — you need to understand it well enough to explain and own it. If you can't, you're not done yet.

This is especially critical in engineering. Do not ship code you don't understand because a model wrote it.

**Letting it flatten your voice**
AI writing tends toward a particular kind of smooth, neutral, slightly over-punctuated prose. It's recognisable, and it's not us. If something you've written sounds like it could have come from anywhere, it probably needs more of you in it.

## AI in client work

Be transparent about how you use AI in client contexts, and use good judgement about when it's appropriate to disclose. In most cases, clients care about the quality of the output and the thinking behind it — not the specific tools used to get there. But if a client asks, be honest.

Never use AI to generate final deliverables without meaningful human review, editing, and ownership. The standard for quality doesn't change because a model was involved.

## AI in code

AI coding assistants — Copilot, Cursor, and similar — are permitted and widely used. They're genuinely useful for boilerplate, repetitive patterns, and generating options to evaluate.

The rules:

- Understand every line before it ships. If you can't read it, don't merge it.
- Review AI-generated code with the same rigour as any other code. More, if anything — models make confident mistakes.
- Don't let AI steer your architecture. Use it for implementation assistance, not structural decisions.
- Keep security in mind. AI tools sometimes suggest insecure patterns without flagging them as such.

## Staying current

AI tooling is moving fast. What we say here will need to be updated. If you think something's out of date, or there's a gap in this guidance that's causing real friction, raise it.

The goal is a set of norms that actually helps — not a document that collects dust.
