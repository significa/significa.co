The all-hands is one of the few moments where the whole company is in the same room at the same time. That makes it worth taking seriously.

We hold an all-hands meeting once a month. It's not a status update or a slide deck marathon — it's a moment to step back from the day-to-day, share what's happening across the company, celebrate what's going well, and address what isn't.

## What it's for

The all-hands serves a few distinct purposes:

**Company-wide visibility.** Projects, clients, and decisions that affect everyone get shared here. People should leave with a clearer picture of where Significa is and where it's heading.

**Recognition.** This is where shoutouts happen — where contributions that might otherwise go unnoticed get acknowledged in front of the whole team. More on that in Shoutouts.

**Alignment.** Key decisions, strategy shifts, or new initiatives are announced and explained here — not buried in a Slack message or saved for one-on-ones.

**Culture.** Being in the room together, even just for an hour, matters. The all-hands is one of the rituals that makes Significa feel like a team and not just a collection of individual contributors.

## Format

All-hands meetings run for approximately **one hour**. The format is consistent month to month, which makes them easier to prepare for and easier to follow.

A typical session includes:

- **Company update** — where things stand commercially, operationally, and strategically.
- **Department highlights** — a brief round from each team on what they've shipped, what's upcoming, and anything worth sharing.
- **Shoutouts** — recognition of individuals or teams for standout contributions.
- **Open floor** — space for questions, announcements, or anything someone wants to raise with the whole company.

The agenda is shared in advance. If you have something to add — an announcement, a topic you want to raise, a win worth celebrating — flag it to whoever is organising that month's session before it goes out.

## Who runs it

All-hands meetings are facilitated by the Partners, though different people may lead different sections. The goal is for it to feel like a team meeting, not a broadcast.

## Attendance

All-hands is mandatory. If you can't attend in person, you join remotely. If there's a genuine conflict, let your Team Lead know in advance.

The value of an all-hands comes partly from everyone being there. When people skip, the room feels it — and so do the people who showed up.

## Coming prepared

You don't need to prepare anything unless your team has a highlight to share or you're presenting something. But you should arrive having thought about whether there's anything you want to raise during the open floor.

The open floor only works if people use it. Questions are welcome. Disagreement is welcome. Silence is the one thing that makes an all-hands feel like a formality rather than a meeting.
