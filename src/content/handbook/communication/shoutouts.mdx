Great work deserves to be named out loud. Shoutouts are how we do that at Significa — a lightweight, public way to recognise a colleague for something they did that made a difference.

It doesn't have to be a heroic effort. It can be as simple as someone who stepped in when a project hit a rough patch, gave unusually honest feedback, or just made the week easier for the people around them. The point is to notice, and to say so.

## Where shoutouts live

Shoutouts happen in our dedicated **#shoutouts** Slack channel. Anyone can post one at any time — no special occasion required, no approval needed.

All-hands meetings are also a moment for shoutouts. If you've been sitting on something worth recognising publicly, that's a good place to bring it.

## How to give a good shoutout

A shoutout lands better when it's specific. "Great job" is kind but forgettable. A shoutout that names what someone actually did — and why it mattered — is the kind that sticks.

A simple structure that works:

**Who** — name the person (or people).
**What** — describe what they did, concretely.
**Why it mattered** — the impact on the project, the team, or the work.

For example:

> Big shoutout to Mariana for jumping into the Faro project during a really hectic week and getting the prototype across the line. She asked all the right questions, moved fast without cutting corners, and kept everyone calm while doing it. That release would not have happened without her.

That's it. No need to overthink it.

## What's worth shouting out

Anything genuinely worth recognising. Some examples:

- Delivering something difficult under pressure
- Going out of their way to support a colleague
- Sharing knowledge that helped the team
- Flagging a problem before it became a real one
- Bringing unusual care or craft to a piece of work
- Making someone's onboarding smoother
- Embodying something from our Culture that's easy to take for granted

If you caught yourself thinking "that was really impressive" or "that made my week easier" — that's your cue.

## Why it matters

Recognition isn't a soft extra. It's part of how a high-performing team stays healthy.

When people feel seen for the right things, they keep doing those things. And when the things being recognised reflect our actual values — accountability, craft, generosity, honesty — it reinforces what good looks like at Significa without anyone having to say it explicitly.

Shoutouts also have a quiet multiplying effect. When someone is recognised publicly for helping a colleague, it signals to everyone else that this kind of behaviour is valued here. Culture is built in these small, repeated moments.

## Give shoutouts freely

Don't wait for the perfect moment. Don't worry that it's too small, or that the person already knows. Say it anyway.

The bar for a shoutout is not "extraordinary achievement." It's "this person did something worth acknowledging." That happens far more often than most of us remember to say so.
