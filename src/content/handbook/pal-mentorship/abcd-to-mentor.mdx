The ABCD framework is how Significa evaluates performance. It's also one of the most useful tools you have in a PAL session — because it gives both mentor and mentee a shared vocabulary for talking about growth, gaps, and what progress actually looks like.

This page is a guide to using ABCD not as an assessment tool, but as a mentorship lens.

## A quick recap of ABCD

The four principles:

- **A — Accountability:** Taking ownership of your work, your commitments, and your impact. Following through, being reliable, and holding yourself to a high standard even when no one is watching.
- **B — Business:** Understanding how your work connects to Significa's commercial reality. Thinking beyond your immediate task to the client relationship, the project's success, and the company's health.
- **C — Culture:** Contributing to the environment we all work in. How you show up for colleagues, how you communicate, how you handle disagreement, and how you embody Significa's values day to day.
- **D — Deliverables:** The quality and consistency of the work itself. Meeting standards, shipping things you're proud of, and continually raising the bar on craft.

Each principle is assessed on a scale of 1 (Entry) to 7 (C-Level), aligned with career levels from Entry through to C-Level. You can read the full breakdown in ABCD performance.

## Why use ABCD in PAL sessions?

Performance reviews happen twice a year. PAL sessions happen every month. That gap is exactly where ABCD mentorship lives.

Rather than waiting for a formal review to understand where you stand, PAL sessions let you use the framework continuously — to reflect on specific situations, identify patterns, and make real adjustments before the next review cycle.

For the mentee, it connects personal development to something concrete and recognised. For the mentor, it provides a structured way to give feedback that isn't just opinion — it's grounded in criteria the whole company understands.

## How to bring ABCD into a session

You don't need to review all four principles every time. Instead, pick the one that's most relevant to what your mentee is working through right now.

A useful opening question: *"If you look at your last few weeks through the ABCD lens, which principle do you feel most confident about — and which one do you feel is lagging?"*

From there, go deep on one.

### Working through a principle together

For whichever principle you're focusing on, try to move through three stages:

**1. Ground it in a specific example**
Abstract conversations about "accountability" or "culture" are hard to act on. Ask your mentee to bring a concrete situation — a project moment, a conversation, a decision — and explore it through the lens of the principle.

*"Tell me about a recent moment where you felt like you really owned something — or where you wish you had."*

**2. Map it to the level descriptors**
Look at the level descriptions together (from Entry to C-Level) and ask your mentee where they honestly see themselves. Then share your perspective.

This isn't about giving a score — it's about getting aligned on what growth in this area actually looks like in practice. The gap between where they are and where they want to be is the development opportunity.

**3. Identify one concrete action**
What's one thing they could do — before the next session — that would represent genuine progress in this principle? It doesn't have to be big. Small, consistent actions compound.

## Principle-by-principle guide for mentors

### Accountability (A)

This principle is often where early-career team members have the most room to grow. The tell-tale signs of underdeveloped accountability are: over-promising, not flagging blockers early, deflecting when something goes wrong, or needing to be chased.

**Questions to explore:**
- When something goes wrong on a project, what's your instinct — to fix it, or to explain it?
- Are there commitments you've made recently that you haven't fully followed through on? What got in the way?
- How quickly do you flag problems to your team or manager? What tends to stop you from doing it sooner?
- What would it look like to take more ownership of [specific area]?

**Signs of growth:**
Proactively communicating risks. Owning mistakes without defensiveness. Following through consistently. Not waiting to be told.

---

### Business (B)

This is often the least visible principle — and the one people are most surprised to be assessed on. It's not just about commercial awareness in the abstract; it's about whether you think beyond your lane.

**Questions to explore:**
- How much do you know about the commercial context of the project you're on right now?
- Have you ever pushed back on a client request or internal decision because you thought it wasn't in the project's best interest? What happened?
- What does Significa's success actually depend on, from your role?
- Are there ways you could contribute more to how we retain or grow client relationships?

**Signs of growth:**
Asking about project profitability and timelines without being prompted. Flagging scope creep. Understanding why decisions are made, not just what was decided. Contributing to pitches or proposals.

---

### Culture (C)

Culture is the most interpersonal of the four principles, and it's also the one people tend to underestimate until they see its impact. It covers how you communicate, how you show up for teammates, and how you contribute to the environment everyone works in.

**Questions to explore:**
- How do you tend to handle disagreement with a colleague or manager?
- Is there someone on the team who you find it harder to work with? What do you think is driving that?
- How often do you actively support or unblock other people — beyond your own workload?
- Are there ways you could contribute more to knowledge sharing, onboarding, or team cohesion?

**Signs of growth:**
Actively amplifying others. Handling conflict directly and constructively. Contributing to team rituals. Being someone people feel comfortable asking for help.

---

### Deliverables (D)

This is the most tangible principle — the quality of the work itself. But "quality" means different things at different levels, and that's exactly what makes it worth exploring.

**Questions to explore:**
- What does your best work look like, and how consistently are you producing it right now?
- Are there areas of your craft where you know you're cutting corners — even slightly?
- What would it mean to raise the standard of your work in [specific area] by one level?
- How do you handle feedback on your deliverables? Does it land easily, or does it tend to feel personal?

**Signs of growth:**
Fewer revision cycles. Proactively seeking quality benchmarks. Taking initiative to improve process, not just output. Producing work that others point to as an example.

## Using ABCD ahead of a review cycle

In the months leading up to a performance review, PAL sessions are especially valuable for helping your mentee prepare.

Use this structure across two or three sessions:

1. **Retrospective:** Look back at the last six months. For each principle, what's the evidence? What moments stand out — positively and negatively?
2. **Gap analysis:** Where are the gaps between current performance and the next level? Are any principles lagging significantly?
3. **Forward plan:** What specific actions, behaviours, or contributions could close those gaps before the review? What should be in their self-reflection?

The goal isn't to game the review — it's to make sure your mentee arrives at it having genuinely grown, and having the language to articulate that growth clearly.

## A note for mentors

Using ABCD as a mentorship tool requires honesty. If you can see that your mentee is clearly underperforming in a principle — and they don't seem to see it — it's your job to say so. Kindly, specifically, and with evidence.

Leaving a blind spot unaddressed isn't kindness. It's the opposite. The most valuable thing a mentor can do is help someone see themselves more clearly — and give them the tools to do something about it.
