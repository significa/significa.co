Feedback is one of the most powerful tools available in a PAL relationship — and one of the most consistently underused. Not because people don't care, but because giving honest feedback feels risky, and asking for it feels vulnerable.

This page is about making both easier.

## Why feedback matters in PAL

The PAL programme exists to accelerate growth. And growth without feedback is mostly guesswork.

You can work hard, show up consistently, and still not improve — if no one tells you what's actually working and what isn't. Feedback is the signal that turns effort into progress.

In a PAL relationship, you have something rare: a regular, trusted space where feedback isn't tied to performance reviews or project pressure. That's exactly the right environment to develop the habit.

## The difference between good and bad feedback

Not all feedback is useful. Bad feedback is vague, personal, or delivered without care. Good feedback is specific, observable, forward-looking, and given in the spirit of the other person's growth.

The difference isn't just in the words — it's in the intent. Before giving feedback, ask yourself: *Am I saying this to help them grow, or to vent, impress, or protect myself?* If it's not the first one, it's worth pausing.

### What good feedback looks like

**Specific, not general**
"Your presentation was unclear" isn't useful. "In the client meeting on Tuesday, the project timeline section lost people — the structure jumped around and it was hard to follow" is.

The more specific the feedback, the more actionable it is. Anchor it to a real moment, a real piece of work, a real behaviour.

**Behavioural, not personal**
Feedback should describe what someone *did*, not who they *are*.

Instead of: "You're not proactive enough."
Try: "In the last three weeks, I've noticed you've waited to be asked before updating the team on blockers. I think flagging those earlier would make a real difference."

One describes a trait. The other describes a pattern that can be changed.

**Forward-looking, not just retrospective**
Good feedback doesn't just diagnose the past — it points toward the future. After naming the observation, say what you'd like to see instead. Make it concrete.

"What I'd find really useful going forward is…"
"One thing that would change the impact of your work is…"

**Timely**
Feedback loses its value when it's delayed. If something happened three months ago and you're only now bringing it up, it's harder to act on and more likely to feel like an ambush.

In a monthly PAL session, there's no excuse for feedback being significantly out of date.

## How to give feedback in a PAL session

### Ask for permission first

Even in an established PAL relationship, it's worth signalling that feedback is coming.

*"There's something I've been wanting to share with you — is now a good time to go into it?"*

This isn't procedural formality. It gives the other person a chance to be mentally ready to receive, which dramatically increases the chance the feedback actually lands.

### Use the SBI model as a scaffold

**Situation — Behaviour — Impact.** A simple, reliable structure.

- **Situation:** When and where did it happen?
- **Behaviour:** What specifically did you observe?
- **Impact:** What was the effect — on the work, the team, the client, or you?

Example:
> *"In last week's project retrospective [Situation], I noticed you interrupted a couple of people mid-sentence when they were giving their input [Behaviour]. I think it made some people hold back in the second half of the session — the energy shifted [Impact]."*

From there, you can open it up: *"I don't know if you were aware of it — what's your take?"*

### Stay curious, not prosecutorial

Feedback isn't a verdict. After you've shared your observation, give the other person room to respond. You might have missed context. They might see it differently. That conversation is part of the value.

The goal is shared understanding, not winning an argument.

### Separate feedback from advice

A common mistake: jumping straight from the observation to telling someone what to do. Give the feedback first, let it land, and then — if it's welcome — move into problem-solving.

*"Would it be helpful to think through how you might approach that differently?"* is a better entry point than immediately prescribing a solution.

## How to ask for feedback

Asking for feedback is a skill in its own right — and it's one most people never develop. Instead, they wait to receive it, which means they're at the mercy of what others decide to share (which is often very little).

### Ask specifically, not generally

"Any feedback for me?" almost never produces useful responses. It puts the burden entirely on the other person to identify something, frame it, and take the risk of delivering it — all at once.

Make it easier by being specific about what you want feedback on.

*"I'd love your honest take on how I handled the client presentation on Thursday. Specifically, how I communicated the design rationale — did it land clearly?"*

*"I've been trying to be more proactive about flagging risks on projects. Have you noticed any change? Is there anything I could do more consistently?"*

Specific requests get specific answers.

### Create safety for honest responses

If you ask for feedback but visibly react badly when you receive it — getting defensive, dismissing it, going quiet — people will stop telling you the truth.

The most important thing you can do after receiving feedback is thank the person for it. Not as a performance, but genuinely. They took a risk to be honest with you. That deserves acknowledgement, even if your immediate reaction is to disagree.

You don't have to agree with feedback to respond to it well.

### Ask more than once

Trust is built over time. In the early sessions of a PAL pairing, people tend to be cautious. The more consistently you ask, respond well, and demonstrate that feedback leads to actual change, the more honest and useful the feedback you'll receive.

### Seek feedback from outside the PAL relationship too

Your PAL partner is one source. But growth requires multiple perspectives. Think about asking your manager, peers on a project, or even a client stakeholder for feedback on something specific.

The PAL session is a good place to process and make sense of feedback you've received from elsewhere, too.

## Receiving feedback gracefully

Receiving feedback well is harder than giving it. Our instinct, when we feel criticised, is to defend — to explain, reframe, or deflect.

That instinct is understandable. It's also the thing that most reliably prevents growth.

### Listen before you respond

When someone gives you feedback, your first job is to understand it — not to react to it. Before you say anything, make sure you've actually heard them.

Reflect back what you heard: *"So what you're saying is that when I do X, the effect is Y — is that right?"* This confirms understanding and gives the other person a chance to clarify.

### Separate the feedback from your feelings about it

Your emotional reaction to feedback and the validity of the feedback are two completely different things.

Something can sting and still be true. Something can feel unfair and still be worth examining. Sit with the discomfort before you decide whether the feedback has merit.

### You choose what to do with it

Receiving feedback well doesn't mean accepting everything uncritically. You're allowed to disagree. But disagreement should come after reflection, not as a reflex.

If you've thought about it and genuinely believe the feedback is off-base, say so — calmly, specifically, and with your own evidence.

*"I hear what you're saying, and I've thought about it. My experience of that situation was different — here's why…"*

That's a mature, useful conversation. Shutting down or going silent isn't.

## Making feedback a habit

The goal, over time, is for feedback to become a natural part of every PAL session — not a special event with extra weight attached.

Start small. At the end of each session, make it routine:

*"Is there anything from today — or from the last few weeks — that would be useful for me to hear?"*

And offer the same in return.

The more ordinary feedback becomes, the easier it is to give honestly and receive openly. That's when it starts to do its real work.
