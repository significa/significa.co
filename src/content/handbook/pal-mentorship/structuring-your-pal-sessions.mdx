A PAL session without structure tends to drift. You catch up, talk about what's been busy lately, and before you know it the hour is gone and neither of you quite knows what you got out of it.

Structure doesn't mean rigidity. It means giving the conversation a shape — a beginning, a middle, and an end — so that the time you invest actually compounds into something.

## How often should you meet?

We recommend a minimum of **once a month**. Twice a month is better, especially in the early stages of a pairing when you're still building rapport and establishing rhythm.

Consistency matters more than frequency. A reliable monthly session is worth far more than sporadic meetings that keep getting rescheduled.

Book your sessions in advance — at least a month out — and treat them as protected time. They're as important as any client meeting.

## Session length

Aim for **45 to 60 minutes**. Shorter than that and you rarely get past the surface. Longer and attention drifts.

If a particular topic needs more space, it's better to schedule a dedicated follow-up than to keep extending an open-ended session.

## Before the session

Good sessions are prepared sessions. Both people should spend a few minutes beforehand thinking about:

- What's been on your mind since the last session?
- Is there a specific challenge, decision, or topic you want to work through?
- Is there anything from last time you want to revisit or close out?

You don't need a formal agenda, but arriving with at least one thing you genuinely want to get into makes a significant difference.

## A session structure that works

There's no single right way to run a PAL session. That said, the following structure is a solid starting point — especially if you're new to the programme or want to reset a pairing that's lost focus.

### 1. Check-in (5 minutes)

Start with a simple human check-in. How are you actually doing — not just professionally? A brief moment to arrive, settle, and signal what kind of headspace you're in.

This isn't small talk. It sets the tone and helps both people shift from the rest of their day into the session.

### 2. Review of the previous session (5 minutes)

Briefly revisit what came out of your last conversation. Did anything shift? Did you act on anything you said you would? What's still open?

This creates continuity. PAL sessions are more useful when they're part of an ongoing thread rather than isolated conversations.

### 3. Main topic (30–40 minutes)

This is the heart of the session. Work through the primary topic or challenge one of you brought.

Some useful approaches depending on what you're working on:

**If it's a challenge or decision** — use the GROW model as a loose scaffold. What's the goal? What's the current reality? What are the options? What will you actually do?

**If it's a development area** — map it against the ABCD framework. Where do you feel strong? Where are the gaps? What would growth look like in this specific area?

**If it's more exploratory** — use the Question bank to guide the conversation. Sometimes the best sessions don't start with a predefined problem.

The mentor's job here is mostly to ask good questions, reflect back what they're hearing, and resist the urge to jump to solutions. The mentee's job is to think out loud, be honest, and stay curious about their own answers.

### 4. Key takeaways and commitments (10 minutes)

Before you close, agree on what came out of the session:

- What was the key insight or realisation?
- Is there anything you're committing to doing before the next session?
- What do you want to carry forward into the next conversation?

Keep this concrete. "I'll think about it" is not a commitment. "I'll spend 30 minutes drafting my development plan by Friday" is.

### 5. Feedback on the session (2–3 minutes)

End with a quick reflection on the session itself. What worked? What could be better next time? Is the structure serving you, or should you try something different?

This habit keeps the pairing healthy and evolving.

## Where to meet

Meet somewhere you can both focus and speak freely. The office is fine for most sessions — a quiet room or a relaxed corner works well. Remote calls are equally valid, especially for remote team members or when schedules make it easier.

What matters is that both people feel comfortable enough to be honest. Avoid noisy or semi-public spaces where one person ends up speaking in half-sentences.

## Keeping notes

It helps to keep a shared, lightweight record of your sessions — nothing formal, just a few lines capturing the main topic, any key insights, and the commitments made.

A shared Notion page or even a simple running doc works fine. The point is to have somewhere to look back, so you can see how far you've come and hold each other accountable to what you said you'd do.

## If a session isn't working

Not every session will be great. Sometimes the timing is off, the energy isn't there, or the conversation doesn't go anywhere useful. That's normal.

If it happens repeatedly, that's worth talking about directly — either within the pairing or with your Team Lead. PAL sessions are meant to be valuable. If they're not, something should change.
