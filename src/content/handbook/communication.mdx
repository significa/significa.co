Good communication isn't about talking more. It's about saying the right things, to the right people, at the right time — and making sure the people who need to hear something actually do.

At Significa, we're a small team, which is an advantage. Information doesn't need to travel far. But small teams can still communicate poorly — in ways that leave people out of the loop, create unnecessary noise, or let important things slip through unacknowledged.

This section covers how we approach communication as a practice: the rituals we've built around it, the tools we use, and the principles we try to hold ourselves to.

## Default to transparency

We share information openly by default. If something is relevant to the team — a project update, a decision, a change in direction — it should be communicated, not assumed. The cost of over-communicating is low. The cost of under-communicating compounds quietly until it becomes a problem.

This applies upwards too. If something is going wrong, if you're blocked, if a client relationship is getting difficult — say it early. The earlier it's said, the easier it is to address.

## Be direct, not blunt

We value directness. At Significa, saying what you actually think is not just permitted — it's expected. Vague or hedged communication wastes everyone's time and often creates more confusion than silence would.

That said, directness is not the same as being blunt or careless with words. You can be honest and still be thoughtful about how and when you say something. Context matters. Tone matters. The goal is for the message to land, not just to be delivered.

## Asynchronous by default, synchronous by choice

Most communication at Significa doesn't need to happen in real time. A well-written message, shared at a reasonable hour, is usually better than a rushed call that interrupts someone's flow.

We default to async — Slack messages, written updates, documented decisions — and reserve synchronous time (meetings, calls, in-person conversations) for things that genuinely benefit from it: complex problems, sensitive conversations, creative sessions that need real-time energy.

If you're scheduling a meeting, ask yourself whether it could be a message instead. If it could, make it a message.
